A field guide to the players you never see

You traded the stock. These people traded around you.

When you buy a share you own a piece of a company — delta one, story over. But most of the volume on your screen, and most of the moves that make no sense, come from players operating behind the quote: dealers hedging options, funds forced to trade by rules, brokers renting you leverage. This is what they actually do all day — and why it moves your stock.

01 · The root mechanism

Dealer gamma: the market's hidden accelerator (and its brakes)

Start where it starts — market making. A market maker is a shop (Citadel Securities, Susquehanna, Jane Street) that stands ready to buy at the bid and sell at the ask, all day, on millions of contracts. They don't want a view on where the stock goes; they want the spread. The whole business is: take the other side of everyone's trade, then neutralize the risk.

In stocks that's easy — sell you 100 shares, buy 100 shares back, flat. In options it's not, and that's where gamma is born.

Why an option can't be hedged once and forgotten

When a dealer sells you a call option, they take on a slice of directional exposure called delta — roughly, how many shares the option behaves like right now. To stay neutral they buy that many shares against it. Fine. But an option's delta changes as the stock moves: a call far below its strike acts like almost nothing (delta ≈ 0); the same call above its strike acts like the stock itself (delta ≈ 1). That rate of change — how fast delta moves as the stock moves — is gamma.

Gamma is the reason a dealer must keep re-hedging, buying and selling the underlying stock continuously just to stand still. Those forced trades are one of the largest flows in the market — and their direction is set by whether the dealer is short or long gamma.

The two worlds

Short gamma (the dealer sold options to the public — the usual state in single-name meme stocks, where retail buys calls): to stay hedged the dealer must buy as the stock rises and sell as it falls — trading with the move. That adds fuel: up begets buying begets up. It's an accelerator. This is the engine of a gamma squeeze.

Long gamma (the dealer owns options — common at the index level, where funds systematically buy puts and sell calls, leaving dealers long gamma): the dealer must sell as the stock rises and buy as it falls — trading against the move. That's a brake: it dampens volatility and can pin a price to a strike near expiry.

The gamma flip
The market isn't permanently in one world. The level where the dealer community's net gamma crosses from positive to negative is the gamma flip. Above it, dealers are long gamma and the tape is calm and mean-reverting; below it, they're short gamma and moves get amplified. Crossing that line is like flipping a switch from shock-absorber to gas-can — the same news produces a shrug on one side and an avalanche on the other.

Real episodes, so it sticks

GameStop, January 2021. A wave of retail call-buying pushed dealers deeply short gamma. As GME rose, they were forced to buy more and more stock to hedge, which pushed it higher, which forced more buying — a textbook gamma squeeze, compounded by an ordinary short squeeze. The stock went from ~$20 to an intraday ~$483.

"Volmageddon," February 2018. For most of 2017 the popular trade was selling volatility, which left dealers long gamma and the S&P eerily calm — the fewest 1% days since the 1960s. When that positioning unwound on Feb 5, 2018, the VIX roughly doubled in a day and Credit Suisse's XIV note lost ~90% of its value overnight: the shock-absorber flipped into an amplifier.

Index pinning, most quiet weeks. Structural put-buying and call-overwriting by institutions leave index dealers long gamma; their against-the-move hedging is a big reason broad indices spend calm periods drifting quietly and gravitating toward big option strikes into monthly expiry.

Why you never saw this as an equities trader: it lives in the options market and in dealer inventory, invisible on a price chart — but it is trading your stock, sometimes as the biggest buyer or seller in the name.
